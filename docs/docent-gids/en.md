# CHAPTER 1 — How to make tutoring a success?

## 1. Quick checklist for every lesson

Use these checklists as a quick reference before each lesson. A trial lesson and a regular lesson require different preparation. Full explanations for each point can be found further in this document.

### Trial lesson checklist

**Before the trial lesson**
- Have I submitted my availability via the link in the WhatsApp message?
- Have I contacted the parent and asked which subject and topics need to be covered?
- Have I read up on the relevant subject matter?
- Have I agreed on where the lesson will take place (for in-person lessons)?
- Have I sent the Google Meet link at least one day in advance (for online lessons)?
- Have I tested my technical setup (for online lessons)?

**During the trial lesson**
- Am I on time, well-presented and approachable?
- Have I introduced myself to the student and to the parent if present?
- Have I created a safe learning environment and made it clear that asking questions is encouraged?
- Have I asked what the student expects from the tutoring?
- Have I maintained a clear lesson structure (theory, example, practice)?
- Am I asking follow-up questions and observing body language?
- Am I keeping track of time? The trial lesson lasts one hour.

**After the trial lesson**
- Have I sent a short message to the student and the parent?
- Have I informed Bright Panda about how the trial lesson went?
- Have I opened my availability on my account?
- Have I proposed a follow-up lesson?

### Regular lesson checklist

**Before the lesson**
- Is the lesson booked on my account before the lesson starts?
- Do I know which subject matter we will cover today and have I prepared for it?
- Have I sent the Google Meet link to the student? You do not need to generate a new link each time — use the same link for all lessons.
- Have I tested my technical setup (for online lessons)?

**During the lesson**
- Am I on time, well-presented and approachable?
- Have I started with a brief check-in: how is the student doing today?
- Have I maintained a clear lesson structure (theory, example, practice)?
- Am I asking follow-up questions and observing body language?
- Am I keeping track of time?
- Have I planned the next lesson together with the student during this lesson?

**After the lesson**
- Have I told the student what we will cover in the next lesson?
- Have I opened my availability on my account for the next lesson?
- Have I noted in two or three sentences what was covered and what is planned for next time?

## 2. How does the matching process work?

[intro] Before you stand in front of a student for the first time, you go through the matching process. This chapter explains how it works, what we expect from you, and why speed and flexibility are so important.

We depend on the requests that come in from parents and students. It may sometimes take a while before we find a suitable match for you. The more flexible you are with your availability, the easier it is for us to find the right match.

We aim to match every student with a tutor within two days. That also means there are no students waiting for you when you first join us — it may take some time before we can link you to the number of students you are looking for. We appreciate your patience and will do our best.

Once we identify a potential match, we reach out to several tutors at the same time to ask whether they would be able to teach that student. We work on a first come, first served basis: the tutor who responds first gets the match. It is therefore important that you respond to our messages quickly. Parents often enquire at multiple agencies at once, and the speed with which we and our tutors act is one of the main reasons we attract and retain clients.

## 4. Preparing for the trial lesson

[intro] Good preparation is half the battle. This chapter walks you through what to do from the moment the trial lesson is scheduled to the start of the lesson. A well-prepared tutor makes a stronger first impression and significantly increases the chance of a successful trial lesson.

Contact the parent as soon as possible. Introduce yourself, ask which topics need to be covered and where the student is struggling. The more information you have in advance, the better you can prepare and the greater the chance the trial lesson is seen as a success. The parent can send materials via WhatsApp.

Read up on the subject matter. A common mistake — and a reason why parents drop out after a trial lesson — is when a tutor does not appear fully confident in the subject. This is easy to prevent by reading up in advance.

Teaching in person? Make sure to agree on where the lesson will take place.

## 5. During the (trial) lesson

[intro] This chapter is the heart of this document. Here you will find everything you need to build and guide a lesson well. Where something applies specifically to the trial lesson, this is indicated. The rest applies to every lesson.

### First impression

Make sure you are on time, ideally five minutes early. A first impression forms incredibly quickly, often within just a few seconds. Non-verbal signals such as your clothing, posture, eye contact and facial expression determine how the student and parent perceive you. Make sure you look presentable and have a friendly, open attitude.

How you present yourself at the moment of introduction says a lot. Make eye contact when you introduce yourself, as this conveys confidence. If you shake someone's hand, give a firm, assured handshake. These may seem like small details, but they are exactly the things that create a strong first impression.

### Build a connection

Start every lesson with a brief moment of connection before moving on to the subject matter. The more at ease the student feels with you, the better the collaboration will be.

At the trial lesson, take more time for this. Introduce yourself to the student and to the parent if they are present, tell them a little about yourself, and ask the student about their interests and hobbies. Not every student is immediately enthusiastic about tutoring, so give it time.

For a regular lesson, a short check-in — how are you, how was your week — is enough to keep the connection alive before you begin.

### Safe learning environment

Make it clear at the start of the lesson that you are there to help the student as well as possible and that it is completely fine if something is not immediately clear. Encourage the student to keep asking questions until they truly understand something. By saying this explicitly, you make it clear that asking questions is not a sign of weakness, but a sign of intelligence.

Also normalise making mistakes. Say explicitly at the start of the lesson that making mistakes is the best way to learn. Deliberately make a mistake yourself and correct it together with the student. This lowers the threshold for trying and creates an environment where the student feels comfortable.

### Expectations and goals

At the start of the lesson — especially the trial lesson — ask what the student expects and how they think they learn best. Be honest that this is a learning process and that you will understand the student better with each lesson.

For a regular lesson, use what you already know: pick up where you left off and ask what the student has practised in the meantime.

### Assertiveness and structure

You are the tutor, so take the lead. Decide which material will be covered in the lesson, review what has already been done and what still needs to be addressed, for example in the run-up to a test. Be clear about whether the planning is realistic or not, and be proactive rather than waiting for the student or parent to come up with a plan.

A structure that works well: go through the theory together, work through an example exercise together, then let the student practise independently. While the student is working, you can observe, step in where needed, and identify which theory still needs more attention.

### Ask follow-up questions and read the student

Not every student will say when they do not understand something. Ask targeted questions and pay attention to body language and facial expressions, as these reveal a lot. Challenge the student as well: 'Great that you understand it, now show me how you would solve this exercise.'

Use the student's name during the lesson. This sounds simple but has a proven effect on engagement and attention. When you address someone by name, they feel seen.

Give the student a degree of choice where possible. Ask open questions rather than closed ones. 'What do you think the next step is?' works better than 'Is this the right step?' An open question forces the student to think, while a closed question invites guessing.

### Active learning and retention

Research shows that students retain information better when they actively engage with it. Ask the student to explain what they have just learned in their own words. If a student can explain something, they truly understand it. This is known as the Feynman technique.

Retrieving information from memory is more effective than re-explaining it. Encourage the student to try and recall how something worked before you explain it again. This significantly strengthens long-term memory. Also let the student know that spending ten minutes a day revisiting covered material is far more effective than studying for a long stretch once a week.

### Building confidence

Many students who need tutoring also have a negative self-image when it comes to the subject. 'I am just bad at maths' is a belief that actively blocks their learning. As a tutor, your job is to break down this image step by step. Celebrate small successes and explicitly point out to the student what they are already doing well.

### Time management

Keep track of time. The trial lesson lasts a maximum of one hour. For regular lessons, stick to the agreed duration. In both cases you may run a few minutes over, but keep it at that.

If the student wants to continue after the trial lesson, they will first need to create an account and purchase a package or subscription through our system. For regular lessons, plan the next lesson preferably at the end of the current lesson so there is no gap.

### Tips for a two-hour lesson

A two-hour lesson requires a different approach. After about 45 minutes, concentration drops significantly, especially for younger students. Plan a short break of five to ten minutes halfway through. Alternate between different activities: start with theory, move to exercises, and close with a brief review or preview of the next lesson.

### Handling an unmotivated student

Not every student is enthusiastic about tutoring. Some are there because their parents require it, others simply do not see the point. Do not get into a power struggle. Connect with the student's interests, make the material relevant to their world and set small, achievable goals. Celebrate small wins — they all count. If a student consistently refuses to engage, contact us so we can work towards a solution together.

### Closing the lesson

At the end of the lesson, ask how the student experienced it and how they see things going forward. Indicate what you will cover in the next lesson.

At the trial lesson: if you briefly spoke with the parent afterwards, give a short update on how it went. This is very much appreciated, especially with younger students, and increases the chance the parent decides to continue.

For a regular lesson: indicate at the end what is planned for next time and, if possible, plan the next lesson then and there.

## 6. Didactic approaches

[intro] There is no single right way to structure a lesson. Depending on the student, the level and the subject matter, one approach may work better than another. Here are four proven approaches.

### Direct instruction

The tutor explains, gives an example, lets the student practise, and provides feedback. This is the most classic approach and works well for VMBO students, students who need structure, and when introducing entirely new material. The structure is clear: first understand, then apply.

### The Socratic method

Instead of giving answers, the tutor asks questions that lead the student to their own insight. This works well with VWO students and with students who already have some knowledge of the material but have not yet fully grasped it. For example: 'What do you think would happen if you skipped this step?' This reinforces understanding far more effectively than explaining, because the student constructs the reasoning themselves.

### The student as teacher

Ask the student to play the role of teacher: have them explain how an exercise works in their own words. This is the Feynman technique in practice and gives you as the tutor immediate insight into what the student does and does not understand. What a student cannot explain well, they have not fully understood.

### Problem-based learning

Start with a concrete exercise or a realistic problem and work back from there to the theory. This is the opposite of the classic approach and works surprisingly well with students who understand the theory but struggle with applying it. It makes the material immediately relevant and tangible.

## 8. When the student becomes a client
[icon: trophy]

[intro] The trial lesson was a success and the student wants to continue. Congratulations! But this is also the moment when the real responsibility begins.

Make sure all lessons are on your account and that your availability is open well in advance. Ideally plan two weeks ahead, so you know where you stand and can support and steer the student in good time. Do not wait until the exam week has already started.

### Tracking progress and communicating honestly

Keep track of what has been covered with each student, what still needs to come, and how the student is developing. Parents pay for results. If lessons start to feel like friendly chats without visible progress, they will stop. Make sure each lesson has a clear goal and that the student knows what has been achieved by the end of it.

We do not expect you to keep detailed reports. What does work and takes little time: write two or three sentences at the end of each lesson noting what was covered and what is planned for next time. This can be a note on your phone, a simple text file or a shared Google Doc with the student.

Ask yourself three short questions after each lesson: what went well, what did the student not fully grasp yet, and what will we pick up next time? That is enough to keep working in a focused and purposeful way.

### When one hour per week is not enough

It sometimes happens that a student has a significant gap in their knowledge but only receives one hour of tutoring per week. If you as a tutor notice this is not enough to bring the student up to the required level within the desired timeframe, it is your responsibility to raise this honestly and in good time, with us and with the parent. Be proactive and honest, even if it is a difficult conversation.

### Do you want to stop with a student?

Give at least one month's notice to both us and the student. This gives us time to find a replacement tutor and ensures the student is not suddenly left without support.

### Holidays, exams or illness

Have you got a holiday, an exam period or another reason why you are less available? No problem, but communicate this clearly and in good time with the parent and student. The quickest way to lose trust is to cancel a lesson at the last minute. Let them know well in advance that you are less available that week and suggest planning a double lesson the week before or after as compensation. Are you ill? No problem, just let us know.

## 9. The physical learning environment

[intro] The environment in which a student learns has a direct impact on their concentration and performance. As a tutor, it is your job to actively manage this, both for in-person and online lessons.

A phone left visible on the table, even if it is not making any noise, measurably reduces concentration. The same applies to a window overlooking a busy street, a TV in the background, or a cluttered workspace. Research confirms that distractions increase cognitive load, leaving less mental capacity for actually processing new information.

As a tutor, you are the one who needs to be assertive about this. At the start of the first lesson, set clear agreements about the learning environment. You can do this in a friendly but direct way: 'I find it works better if we both put our phones away for now, so we can really focus.' By framing it as a shared agreement rather than an imposed rule, the student is more likely to go along with it.

Teaching in person and the student is sitting near a window or another source of distraction? Suggest changing seats. For online lessons the same applies: ask the student to sit in a quiet spot and put their phone away. Do the same yourself. Lead by example.

## 10. Teaching online

[intro] Teaching online offers a lot of flexibility, but also requires solid preparation. Technical problems, a poor connection or a distracting environment can completely derail a lesson.

Before the lesson, you can request a Google Workspace account via Bright Panda, which allows you to set up Google Meet sessions. Ask for this in good time. Send the meeting link at least one day in advance, as parents want to know where they stand and do not want to be scrambling an hour before the lesson. Also test your microphone, camera, audio and internet connection beforehand, and make sure you are in a quiet spot without background noise.

During the lesson, make sure your camera is on and your environment is quiet. Background noise is very distracting for the student, so use a good microphone.

### Useful tools for online teaching

With Google Docs you can create a document and share it with the student so you can both work in it at the same time. Make sure the student has editing rights. Miro (miro.com) is a free digital whiteboard you can share with the student, great for working through exercises. If you have a tripod for your phone, you can easily point your camera at your notebook to work through a problem.

## 11. Working smarter with online tools

[intro] You do not need to reinvent the wheel every lesson. A few free tools make your lessons clearer and save you time.

Set up a Google Meet link once and use it for all lessons with the same student. You do not need to generate a new link each time. Send the link at the first lesson, communicate this clearly to the parent and student, and save it yourself. That way the student always knows where to go.

Also create a shared Google Doc as a lesson log. Note down after each lesson in two or three sentences what was covered and what is planned for next time. Give the student editing rights so they can look back at any time. Completed exercises can be saved directly in the document, so both you and the student can always refer back to them. It works as a shared memory for the entire tutoring period.

Miro (miro.com) is a free online digital whiteboard that you can share with the student. Everything you draw or write is immediately visible to the student in real time, and vice versa. You can download the app on both your laptop and your phone. On your phone you can draw with your finger, which is ideal for quickly sketching a graph or writing out a formula. Set up a board once, share the link with the student and reuse it across multiple lessons. Get started at miro.com and create a free account.

## 12. Teaching different age groups and levels

[intro] The age and school level of a student strongly influence how you as a tutor can best guide them. This chapter covers the most important insights and tips per group.

### Lower secondary school and primary school

Younger students need more structure and shorter concentration blocks. They are more sensitive to your tone and attitude than older students. Positive reinforcement is most effective here. Keep explanations short and concrete and use plenty of examples from their daily lives. Game elements or a light element of competition can help increase engagement. Be patient and give the student time to open up, especially in the first lesson.

### Upper secondary school and exam years

Students in the upper years are more capable of abstract thinking and can handle longer explanations. They are often under more pressure due to approaching exams. Be realistic about what is achievable and help them prioritise. These students also benefit more from learning good study strategies, not just understanding the material itself.

### VMBO

VMBO students learn best through concrete, practical and shorter tasks. Abstract theory without direct application loses them quickly. Always use examples from everyday life and work with plenty of repetition and small steps. VMBO students also more frequently struggle with lower self-confidence when it comes to learning. Be patient and build trust before expecting them to work independently. Every small step forward is a win worth acknowledging.

### HAVO

HAVO students can think more abstractly but still benefit from concrete reference points. For HAVO students, motivation is often the biggest challenge, not ability. Make the material as relevant as possible, show them why they are learning it and maintain a clear structure. Give them responsibility for their own learning process but actively guide that process.

### VWO and Gymnasium

VWO students are often used to a fast pace and high expectations. The challenge here is sometimes the opposite: they expect to understand everything immediately and struggle when they do not. Teach them that effort and making mistakes are part of learning, not a sign of failure. With VWO students you can go deeper into the material and probe their reasoning and justification.

## 13. Teaching students with specific learning needs

[intro] We work exclusively with underage students. Some of them have a diagnosis or learning difficulty that requires an adapted approach. Below you will find practical tips per situation.

### ADHD and ADD

Students with ADHD or ADD struggle with sustained concentration. Break the lesson into shorter blocks of ten to fifteen minutes with a brief change of activity in between. Use concrete and visual examples and avoid long theoretical explanations. Give clear, short instructions and repeat them if necessary. Ensure a calm environment free from distraction and actively ask the student to put their phone away. Positive reinforcement works far better than correction. A useful technique is the Pomodoro method: twenty to twenty-five minutes of focused work followed by a five-minute break.

### Dyslexia

Students with dyslexia process written information more slowly. Read texts aloud and let the student follow along while listening. Use larger fonts when working digitally. Give more time for reading and writing exercises and never judge on spelling errors. Focus on understanding rather than accurate copying. Colour-coding information can help with structuring subject matter.

### Dyscalculia

Students with dyscalculia struggle with understanding numbers and mathematical concepts. Use visual aids such as a number line, blocks or diagrams. Always link mathematics to concrete, recognisable situations from everyday life. Work through things step by step and make sure the student truly understands each part before moving on. Avoid time pressure when working through problems.

### Students who are easily distracted

Ensure a fixed structure at the start of each lesson: what are we going to do today and why. Alternate between different types of activities, such as explanation, independent practice and discussion. Keep tasks short and manageable. Ask the student to put their phone away and do the same yourself. Use a timer as a tool to create focus.

## 14. Practical scenarios

[intro] No matter how well you prepare, you will encounter situations as a tutor that you did not expect. This chapter covers a number of common situations with a practical approach for each one.

### The student says they understand, but then gets the exercise wrong

Challenge the student in a friendly way: 'Great, then show me how you would approach this exercise.' Go through the steps together and ask questions as you go. You will find exactly where the understanding breaks down. Then go back to that specific point and cover it again, possibly with a different explanation or example.

### The student cannot put their phone down

Turn it into a shared agreement: 'Shall we both turn our phones face down for the next twenty minutes so we can really focus?' If the behaviour continues, address it calmly: 'I notice the phone keeps taking your attention. Do you think it would help to pop it in your bag?'

### The student genuinely does not understand the material no matter how you explain it

Stop repeating the same explanation in the same way. Try a completely different approach: use a drawing, an analogy from everyday life, a different example or a different working method. Sometimes it helps to ask the student what they do already understand, and build from there. If it still does not work, make a note and contact us.

### A parent gives comments during the lesson

Stay calm and professional. Thank the parent for the input: 'Thank you, I will certainly take that on board.' Then continue with the lesson. If the interference is ongoing and disrupting the lesson, discuss it with the parent afterwards in a friendly way. Contact us if the problem persists.

### A student becomes emotional or cries

Stop the lesson content for a moment. Give the student space and show that you are there to help, not to judge. Say something like: 'That is completely fine, take a moment.' Then calmly ask what is going on. Do not force anything. If you are concerned about the student, let us know after the lesson.

### A student has a large knowledge gap just before an exam

Be honest about what is realistic. Discuss with the student and parent which areas are the priority and which goals are achievable in the time available. Make a concrete plan and communicate clearly what you will cover each lesson. Make clear that miracles are not possible, but that working in a targeted way can make a real difference.

### A parent asks you to plan lessons outside of Bright Panda

Respond in a friendly but clear manner: 'I understand you would like to reduce costs, but I am linked to you through Bright Panda and all lessons go through their platform. That is the agreement we both have with them. I would suggest contacting Bright Panda if you have any questions about the rates.' Let us know about the conversation afterwards.

## 15. Lesson cancellations
[icon: canceled]

[intro] It happens that a student or parent needs to cancel a lesson. That is fine, but there are clear agreements in place.

### Cancellation less than 24 hours in advance

When a student or parent cancels a lesson less than 24 hours before it is due to start, that lesson will be charged. You as a tutor have kept your schedule free and reserved your time for that lesson. That deserves respect.

We understand that unforeseen circumstances do arise. In that case we ask you as a tutor to show some flexibility and let it go the first time. Communicate this in a friendly but clear way to the parent: 'I will make an exception this time, but I would ask you to keep in mind for the future that lessons cancelled less than 24 hours in advance will be charged.' Do not let it go a second time. Also let us know so that we can step in with the parent if needed.

### Cancellation by the tutor

Of course the same applies to you as a tutor. Try to cancel lessons as early as possible if you are unavailable. Stick to the 24-hour boundary yourself and communicate in good time. See also section 8 on holidays, exams and illness.

# CHAPTER 2 — Code of Conduct

As a tutor at Bright Panda you represent our company. How you conduct yourself towards students, parents and us as an organisation has a direct impact on our reputation and your own position. This chapter gives you clarity on what we expect from you and how to handle situations you may encounter.

## 1. Professionalism and conduct

At Bright Panda we welcome everyone, regardless of background, religion, sexual orientation, gender identity or disability. Diversity makes our team stronger. We ask the same of you: treat every student and parent with respect, without distinction.

Tutoring is a professional context. The lesson is about the student and their learning goals. It is not a place to promote your own beliefs, political opinions or personal views. Stay focused on the subject and respect the student's space.

### Communication with parents

Your phone number is shared with the parent purely and solely for scheduling lessons and communicating about lesson progress. Keep all communication professional and businesslike. Do not send messages to students outside the context of the lessons.

### Lessons outside Bright Panda are strictly prohibited

Both you as a tutor and the parent enter into an agreement with Bright Panda at the start. It is therefore strictly prohibited to plan or give lessons outside our platform to students who have been matched with you through Bright Panda. This also applies if a parent asks you directly.

Bright Panda invests a great deal in finding students, matching tutors and safeguarding quality. Making use of that service and then booking lessons outside our platform means you are benefiting from our investment without giving anything in return. We consider this theft.

If this is discovered, the contract will be terminated with immediate effect. In addition, we reserve the right to impose additional sanctions, including claiming damages.

As a tutor you sometimes come to know information about a student or family: home situation, academic performance, personal problems. This is confidential information.

Never share information about a student or parent with third parties. Do not discuss students by name on social media or in other public contexts.

It can happen that during a lesson you experience something that makes you feel uncomfortable. This can be anything: an inappropriate remark, a student behaving strangely, or a parent putting you in an awkward position.

Stay calm and professional. Do not react impulsively. If appropriate, calmly and clearly state that you are uncomfortable with the situation. End the lesson in a proper way if the situation requires it. Then contact us as soon as possible via WhatsApp or phone. You never have to deal with this alone.

## 5. Confrontations with students or parents

In exceptional cases a conflict or confrontation may arise. Always stay calm and do not raise your voice. Listen to what the other person is saying without interrupting. Do not defend yourself immediately — first acknowledge the other person's feelings and indicate that you want to resolve the situation. Then contact us so we can help mediate. We do not expect you to resolve these situations yourself.

## 8. Appearance and presentation

As a tutor at Bright Panda you come to students' homes or teach online. How you look has a direct impact on how a parent and student perceive you, especially at the first meeting. We do not require a dress code, but we do ask that you look neat and presentable.

In practice this means, but is not limited to: clean clothing that suits a professional context. No deep necklines, no exposed midriff, no visible underwear and no offensive prints. The same applies online — parents and students see you on camera.

Religious clothing or head coverings, tattoos and piercings are fully welcome, as long as the overall appearance is neat and presentable.

### Personal hygiene

Tutoring means sitting close to someone. Make sure you are fresh and that your clothing does not smell of sweat. Also pay attention to your breath: coffee, garlic and cigarettes can cause unpleasant odours. Brush your teeth before the lesson or use a breath freshener. Well-kept nails are part of this too.

This may seem obvious, but it has a direct impact on how comfortable a lesson is for the student.

## 9. Use of tools and AI

We encourage the use of smart tools in a responsible way. Via your Bright Panda Google Workspace for Education account you have access to a privacy-secured Google environment. Within this environment you can use Gemini (Google's AI assistant), NotebookLM and other Google services. Everything you enter there falls under the privacy agreements of Google Workspace for Education — your data is not used to train AI models.

Practical use: Gemini can help you prepare explanations, come up with examples or find a different approach for a student who is not grasping something. NotebookLM can be used to analyse teaching materials or summaries. Use these tools as preparation, not as a replacement for your own insight.

What you do not do: have AI complete homework or tests for a student, or read out AI-generated explanations without understanding them yourself. The student is paying for your guidance, not a chatbot.
